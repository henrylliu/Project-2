Proposition: A country's economic strength plays a role in CO2 emissions

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Top emitters were more of a warning color, using colors such as orange, yellow, and pink to show that this is in a sense the “danger area” for CO2. We tried avoiding red and green in consideration of the colorblind. Score: -0.5
  • We originally wished to pick top and bottom countries in GDP, but that would appear as if the data was extremely cherry-picked. To combat this, we added more countries to the visualization. Score: 2
  • We decided to use a log transformation along the Y axis for both GDP and CO2 emissions. This was done to reflect the fact that the highest and lowest in both of these categories are several magnitudes apart from each other as well as the transformation in these figures through time. To plot a linear scale would make it hard to visualize the scale of GDP and CO2 for each country. Score: 2

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • We used different year ranges between the plots. The reason for this is it made the point clearer that there is no relation between GDP and CO2 emissions. Score: -2
  • Both axes are actually on the same scale. This was done to make the point clearer and more concise. Score: 2
  • We plotted the top 3 Countries (USA, Germany, Japan) and the bottom 3 Countries (Marshall Island, Tuvalu, Kiribati) in terms of gross GDP. We did this to show that this trend exists for countries across the whole range of GDPs. Score: 2

Final Reflection

Creating both visualizations revealed how easily design and framing can influence interpretation even though we were working with the same data. The FOR visualization felt more straightforward and earnest, as the only deceptive design choice that we employed was the use of color country emissions. The AGAINST visualization, however, required more deliberate design choices (e.g., differing time ranges, selective focus) to make it appear visually compelling. This exercise made us aware of how small aesthetic or structural tweaks such as axis limits and colors can make a huge difference in how we perceive the visualizations.

Ethical visualization now feels less about avoiding persuasion altogether and more about being transparent about what’s being emphasized. Visuals can both reveal and conceal patterns. We’ve learned that acceptable persuasion clarifies data insights, while misleading design manipulates perception without disclosure. If the viewer can reasonably reconstruct the underlying reality from your chart, your design is ethical.
